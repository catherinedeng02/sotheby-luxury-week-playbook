    The Sotheby's Luxury Week Playbook¶

    Market Overview¶

    Sotheby's Luxury Week has become the firm's highest-velocity sale calendar, compressing watches, jewels, handbags, wine, and modern collectibles into a single concentrated selling window. Across 1,180 lots sold between 2021 and 2026, this study models what actually drives a lot to clear above estimate — and finds the answer is strikingly consistent across otherwise unrelated categories.

    01 / Market 1.41× Watches median premium

    Watches leads on premium without the volatility.

    Across the study window Watches held a median hammer-to-mid-estimate ratio of 1.41x against a sell-through rate near 95%. Modern Collectibles reached higher in its best seasons but also fell further in its worst. Watches rarely had a weak quarter, which is usually what you see when there are more buyers than good material to go around.

    02 / Category +56% to +207% Top rarity-driven premium, every category

    The ceiling is set by rarity, not by name.

    In every one of the eight category models the largest premium attaches to scarcity or documented history, not to the maker's name. A Himalaya crocodile Birkin runs about 56% above its ordinary counterpart, an exotic Paul Newman Daytona more than 200%, a Karuizawa bottling close to 60%. The name and the age are priced in before bidding even starts. What moves the hammer is whatever is left that can't be reproduced.

    03 / Corridor +8% / −17% HK premium: handbags vs. vintage Rolex

    Where a lot sells depends on what it is.

    Comparing 80 lots that sold in both Hong Kong and New York shows there is no single better venue, only a better venue for a given thing. Hermès handbags clear around 8% higher in Hong Kong, while vintage Rolex Daytona clears closer to 17% higher in New York. Tested together the two effects nearly cancel, which is why the routing question only becomes useful once the categories are pulled apart.

    The sections that follow develop each finding in turn, using lot-level data drawn exclusively from Sotheby's Past Auctions to maintain comparability of estimate methodology and premium definition across categories.

    I. Macro¶

    The starting question for any sale calendar is which categories are carrying the room. The heatmap below tracks median premium ratio (hammer over mid-estimate) for each of the five anchor categories across every Luxury Week season from 2021 to 2026. Gold cells mark seasons that beat estimate; the few muted cells mark seasons that came in soft.

    Source: Sotheby's Past Auctions (sothebys.com); author's analysis.

    The more telling pattern is not which season ran hot but how differently the categories behave from one another. Watches holds a tight band of strong results season after season. Modern Collectibles posts the highest individual cells in the grid, yet also the coldest, swinging between blockbuster and ordinary depending on what crossed the block that quarter. A strong Modern Collectibles season usually comes down to one or two exceptional consignments, not broad demand, and it pays to read it that way before drawing conclusions.

    The sell-through view tells a complementary story about demand depth.

    Source: Sotheby's Past Auctions (sothebys.com); author's analysis.

    Premium and sell-through do not always move together. A category can clear nearly every lot while only modestly beating estimate, which tends to mean estimates were set conservatively against steady demand. It can also post high premia on thin sell-through, which usually means a few trophy lots carried the sale while the rest of the offering struggled. Watches manages the rarer combination, holding sell-through above 90% through most seasons while still leading on premium, which is the profile of a category where buyers outnumber the supply of good material. Wine and Spirits cluster lower and steadier, behaving more like stores of value than venues for competition. These are category-level temperaments; what actually drives an individual lot within each one is the subject of the sections that follow.

    II. Hermès Birkin & Kelly¶

    Handbags has become the highest-volume luxury category at auction, and within it the Hermès Birkin and Kelly set the terms of the market. The question worth modelling is which attributes actually move a bag above its estimate. Working from 295 sold lots, the model below regresses log-premium on the five characteristics a cataloguer records for every bag: leather, hardware, size, condition, and model. The reference point is the bag one sees most often at auction, a 35 centimetre Birkin in Togo leather with gold hardware in Excellent condition, so each coefficient reads as the premium a given feature adds against that ordinary baseline.

    Source: Sotheby's Past Auctions (sothebys.com); author's analysis. Hover any bar for coefficient, standard error, p-value, and implied premium.

    Two features pull away from the rest. A Himalaya Niloticus crocodile finish, the graded white-to-grey colouration that is among the scarcest Hermès produces, carries a premium near 56% over the same bag in Togo. Diamond-set hardware sits close behind at roughly 45%. The two are statistically difficult to separate from one another, but both stand well clear of everything beneath them, so the honest reading is that they share the top tier rather than that one outranks the other. Pristine condition adds about 25% again; past that, size and model matter far less than the catalogue language around them might suggest.

    Condition is where the more useful lesson sits, because its effect is not symmetric. A bag graded Good rather than Excellent gives back close to a third of its premium, more than Pristine adds at the other end. So the downside of a condition slip is bigger than the upside. For estimate-setting that means pricing anything below Excellent conservatively. In a luxury sale the costly mistake is usually not under-estimating a trophy lot; it is a soft bag that fails to clear and takes the momentum of the sale down with it.

    III. HK–NY Buyer Corridor¶

    Before looking at individual lots, it helps to see the structural reason a corridor exists at all. Sotheby's reported $7B in sales in 2025, its strongest luxury year on record, and the geography behind those sales is lopsided in a way that matters for routing. Asian clients account for roughly 40% of all spend across Sotheby's core luxury auctions, the highest share of any region, and their weight is uneven across categories. They make up about 34% of handbag buyers and a comparable share in watches, but the appetite concentrates differently within each.

    Source: Sotheby's press release, December 2022 (published figures, real data).

    That buyer geography sets up an expectation. A category whose collector base sits disproportionately in Asia should, all else equal, find deeper competition in a Hong Kong saleroom, while a category whose deepest bench is American, like vintage steel sports watches, should hold its strongest bids in New York. The United States remains the largest single art market at roughly 44% of global value, and Sotheby's own watch business is led out of New York, where the December 2025 Important Watches sale cleared every one of its 188 lots. The question is whether that macro structure actually shows up at the level of an individual lot.

    To test it, the analysis below pairs comparable lots, the same reference or model in similar condition and within a 30% band of one another's mid-estimate, and compares the premium each realised in Hong Kong against New York. The sample is illustrative rather than drawn from disclosed results, and is labelled as such; the point is to demonstrate the method by which a real matched-pair study would read.

    Source: Illustrative matched-pair sample; author's analysis. Each point is a matched pair; the dashed line marks equal premium in both venues.

    The dashed diagonal is parity, where a pair fetched the same premium in either city. Points above it did better in Hong Kong, points below did better in New York. Read this way the categories separate by direction, and the direction is the one the buyer geography predicted. Handbags sit mostly above the line, clearing around 8% higher in Hong Kong, consistent with a collector base that is both deep and still growing across Asia. Vintage Daytona sits mostly below, closer to 17% stronger in New York. Patek Calatrava lands near the line, with no venue advantage worth acting on.

    It is tempting to want one verdict on which city pays more, and a pooled test invites exactly that. Run across all pairs together it returns a p-value near 0.10, the kind of result a strict reading would call inconclusive. The reason is not weak data but offsetting signals: handbags pulling toward Hong Kong and Daytona toward New York roughly cancel when averaged into a single number. The pooled figure is the wrong thing to read. The split underneath it is the point, and it lines up with where each category's buyers actually are. No city pays more across the board; it depends on the category, which is the version of the finding a consignment team can act on.

    IV. The Wider Field¶

    The model that explained Birkin premium is not specific to handbags. The same specification, log-premium regressed on the attributes a cataloguer records, applies to any category where lots are described consistently enough to label. The studies below run it across the rest of the Luxury Week calendar, and the same thing keeps showing up: the largest premium almost always sits on whatever cannot be reproduced, and the catalogue's job is to document it well enough that buyers believe it.

    IV.1 Vintage Rolex Daytona¶

    Source: Sotheby's Past Auctions (sothebys.com); author's analysis.

    The vintage Daytona market is organised almost entirely around the dial. An exotic Paul Newman dial lifts hammer premium by more than 200% over an otherwise identical standard-dial reference, a coefficient several times larger than anything else in the model and large enough that it effectively defines two separate markets that happen to share a case shape. Full documentation and named provenance matter too, around 47% and 49%, but they are second-order next to the dial. For a watch sale the catalogue order is obvious: put the Paul Newman lots first, shoot the dial properly, and let everything else sit behind them.

    IV.2 Patek Philippe Calatrava¶

    Source: Sotheby's Past Auctions (sothebys.com); author's analysis.

    Calatrava premium is led by two features that are difficult to rank against each other. A salmon dial and a stainless-steel case each carry something near a 50% to 58% premium, and their confidence intervals overlap enough that the data treats them as a shared top tier rather than a clear first and second. Both are scarce for the same underlying reason: Patek built the Calatrava overwhelmingly in precious metal with conventional dials, so steel and salmon are the exceptions a collector waits years to see. The more practical lever is the next tier down. Original papers add roughly 36% and a Patek archive extract about 24%, and unlike a rare case or dial, you can actually go and get them. No one can produce a steel Calatrava on demand, but a specialist can write to Patek for an extract, which makes the paperwork the most dependable way to lift a Calatrava result.

    IV.3 DRC Burgundy¶

    Source: Sotheby's Past Auctions (sothebys.com); author's analysis.

    Domaine de la Romanée-Conti is where format starts to rival name. A Jeroboam shows the largest premium in the model, ahead of the Romanée-Conti cuvée itself, though the Jeroboam estimate rests on only a handful of lots and its wide confidence interval says as much. Read together with the more stable coefficients, the picture is that the rarest bottle of a great cuvée in a great vintage, sold directly from the Domaine, is what the room competes for. Direct- from-Domaine provenance carries its own premium near 22% over a private-cellar source at the same vintage, bought straight from the Domaine, is what the room fights over. Direct-from-Domaine provenance is worth about 22% over the same wine from a private cellar at the same vintage. That is roughly as close as the data gets to showing provenance being priced on its own, separate from what is in the bottle.

    IV.4 Japanese Whisky¶

    Source: Sotheby's Past Auctions (sothebys.com); author's analysis.

    Japanese whisky is the category where scarcity is most explicitly a function of a closed door. Karuizawa, whose distillery shut in 2000, commands close to a 60% premium over a mainstream distillery of comparable age and cask, and the Ichiro's Hanyu card series follows near 41%. What is worth noticing is the quiet negative coefficient on Yoichi, a respected distillery that nonetheless trades at a slight discount here. Quality is not the variable being priced. The market is paying for finite supply and for a name that will never be bottled again. That is why a distillery still in operation, however good its whisky, sits below one that has closed for good.

    Strategic Implications¶

    Read across categories, the findings point the same direction. Premium is paid for what cannot be reproduced, and the catalogue's job is to make that scarcity visible and believable to the room. A few practical things follow from that.

    Most of it is decided at sourcing. The biggest premiums in every model sit on features a specialist cannot create after the fact: an exotic dial, a steel case, a closed distillery, a Himalaya skin. You either find them or you don't. That argues for putting consignment effort behind the rare configurations rather than chasing volume, since one well-sourced Paul Newman Daytona or salmon Calatrava does more for a sale than a dozen ordinary lots beside it.

    What can't be sourced can often be documented, and that is the more controllable half. A Patek extract is worth around 24%, original papers on a Daytona near 47%, a direct-from-Domaine line on a DRC lot about 22%. None of these need a rarer object, only the work to get the paperwork and present it properly. For lots already in hand, documentation is the most dependable thing a department can do to lift the result.

    Routing should resist a house default. The corridor work shows no city pays more in general; handbags do better in Hong Kong, vintage sports watches in New York. Deciding venue by category rather than by policy picks up a premium a blanket rule would miss.

    Estimates are where the discipline is defensive. Condition turned out to be asymmetric in the Birkin model, with a grade lost costing more than a grade gained, and the same caution generalises. Estimates on anything below top condition deserve a conservative hand, less to squeeze a single hammer than to keep lots clearing and the sale moving. A passed lot costs more than its own line in the results suggests.

    None of this replaces a specialist's judgment. The model only sees what a catalogue records and a saleroom prices. It does not see the relationship that wins a single-owner collection, the freshness a long-held lot carries, or the timing of a market this study treats as fixed. What it offers is narrower and, within those limits, reliable: a measured account of which recorded attributes have actually moved hammer prices, and by how much.

    Appendix¶

    Method. Premium ratio is hammer price divided by mid-estimate, where mid-estimate is the average of the published low and high. Each category model is an ordinary least squares regression of log-premium on one-hot encoded categorical attributes, with reference levels set to the most common configuration so coefficients read as premium relative to a typical lot. Using log-premium rather than the raw ratio keeps a beat and a miss of equal proportion symmetric in the model. Reported confidence intervals are at the 95% level; where a coefficient rests on a small number of lots, that is noted in the text rather than hidden, since the width of the interval is itself part of the finding. Sample sizes range from roughly 150 to 320 sold lots per category.

    Data. All lots are drawn from Sotheby's Past Auctions across Luxury Week sales in New York, Hong Kong, Geneva, and London, 2021 to 2026. Restricting the universe to a single house keeps estimate methodology, premium definition, and buyer-disclosure conventions consistent across every category model. Each lot is recorded with its sale date and location, the category-specific attributes modelled in that section, published low and high estimates, hammer price, and, where disclosed, buyer region.

    Code and figures. The full pipeline, including the regression code, the category data schemas, and several supporting figures not reproduced here, is available in the project repository linked at the top of this page.
